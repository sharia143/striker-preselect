Does a striker model built on one league work in another?

The shortlist model was trained on La Liga 2015/16, then tested on the full Premier League and Serie A of the same season — plus a spotlight on modern squads. The finding is clean: the ranking travels across leagues; the decision threshold does not.

Read it honestly. Logistic regression wins on calibration in all three leagues (robust across 50 CV seeds) — but gradient boosting is a weak comparator at n≈100, so this argues for simplicity, not that any model is strongly calibrated (Serie A's Brier is poor in absolute terms). La Liga's higher scoring bar is real in-sample but, with ~100 players per league, its confidence interval overlaps the others — descriptive, not proof of a tougher league.

03 — THE HEADLINE

The ranking transfers. The threshold doesn't.

Train the scaler + logistic model on La Liga only, then score another league's forwards. Two very different things happen to the two halves of the model.

The further a league sits from La Liga's scoring profile, the fewer of its genuinely-elite strikers survive the fixed cutoff. The order is preserved; the calibrated line in the sand is not portable.

Why it breaks. The model is npxG-dominated (coefficient +1.37) and slightly penalises raw shot volume (−0.20). Serie A's top-quartile scorers actually take more shots and get more box touches than La Liga's — but post lower npxG (0.33 vs 0.40) because they finish above expectation. An npxG-driven model therefore under-rates them. This is a concrete, quantified version of the project's "calibrated to one league only" limitation.

04 — WHAT "ELITE" MEANS

Each league has a different striker fingerprint

Standardised logistic-regression coefficients — how much each per-90 signal predicts top-quartile output. npxG matters everywhere; the rest diverges.

La Liga Premier League Serie A

La Liga rewards chance quality (npxG towers; shot volume is even slightly negative). The Premier League uniquely rewards creation — key passes is its single largest coefficient anywhere. Serie A rewards efficient finishing with everything else muted. Pressures are negative in all three: elite scorers press less. (Small-sample caveat applies to the coefficients too.)

06 — HOW TO READ THIS

What it does — and doesn't — show

The comparison is only as trustworthy as its caveats. These are stated up front, because knowing the limits is the point of a calibrated model.

Descriptive, not predictive

The target (npGA/90) and a core feature (npxG/90) are both same-season output measures and correlate ≈0.72. The model partly re-describes a cousin of its own target. It characterises the profile of a high-output striker; it does not forecast future or transfer value.

Small samples

95–109 qualifying forwards, only 24–28 "elite" per league. Every statistic carries wide intervals; most cross-league gaps sit within sampling noise. Treat differences as directional.

Low minutes floor

The 450-minute cut lets small-sample per-90 leaders (e.g. Gabbiadini, 621') outrank higher-volume proven scorers. The model rewards rate over volume.

Weak baseline

Gradient boosting is a straw comparator at n≈100. "Logistic regression wins" means the simple model is the right default here — not that performance is strong in absolute terms.
